Problems 29–33 highlight something very interesting in relation to programming.

Often in programming, once we have written a failing test, made it pass through implementation, and reached the refactoring stage, we spend time rearranging code to find the clearest or most elegant version.

Sometimes we think we have considered many, most, or even all of the best alternatives, only for someone else to come along and express the same idea more elegantly.

Problems 29–33 demonstrate this nicely. As you work through them, you begin to recognize sequences generated by raising `z` to some power, or by raising a multiple of `z` to some power.

By problem 33, you might first write:

`{ z⁻³ : z ∈ ℕ }`

Then you realize that the same set can be written more transparently as:

`{ 1/z³ : z ∈ ℕ }`

The two expressions are mathematically equivalent, but the second makes the pattern—reciprocals of cubes—more immediately apparent.

I mention this because the sequence of problems from 29–33 captures the same feeling that often occurs while refactoring code: you think you have found the best expression, and then someone else comes along and improves it.
